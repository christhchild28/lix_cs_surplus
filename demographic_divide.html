The Access Problem Gets Worse with Younger Demographics

66% of viewers under 30 don't have cable access. These are exactly the demographics advertisers pay premiums to reach — young, digitally engaged consumers building brand loyalty. Yet two-thirds are locked out of watching the Super Bowl.

Interest vs. Access: Not the Same Problem

Only 23% of Gen Z identify as "avid sports fans" (vs 42% of Millennials). But that's a separate issue from the 65% who are cord-cutters. Among Gen Z who CAN watch, viewership grew 20–24% year-over-year — proving that access, not interest, is the binding constraint.

When They Can Access It, Young Viewers Watch

The Market Failure: Demand Exists, But Access Doesn't

Female viewers 18–24 showed 24% year-over-year growth, and male viewers 18–24 showed 20% growth. This proves latent demand exists. The ~103 million locked-out viewers aren't uninterested — they're structurally excluded from the market.

Advertisers Pay More, Get Less

Super Bowl advertisers pay ~$8M per 30-second spot to reach 125.6M viewers — a CPM of ~$6,369. In an efficient market with the full ~228M potential audience reachable, that same spend would yield a CPM of ~$3,503. Access barriers inflate the effective CPM by 82%, meaning advertisers overpay for every impression while ~$655M in reachable ad revenue simply ceases to exist.
